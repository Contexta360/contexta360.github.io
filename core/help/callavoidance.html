For cost-conscious customer service organisations, creating self-service tools on their website, phone menus (IVR), mobile apps and community forums are a cost-effective way to deflect customers from the most expensive channel - the contact centre agent. And whilst each of these channels is designed with the best intentions, not every scenario can be accounted for at design time. Although usually the objective is to increase call avoidance and channel containment, some revenue-generating centres will be trying to do the opposite, for example, monitoring shopping cart abandonment to see why customers leave their items and call.

Call Avoidance is trained with the Topic Labeller. Segments that contain call avoidance are detected. Therefore, you will see events when call avoidance is present (below target).
Call Avoidance can enable users to try and understand broken processes. An example is when a call should not have got to a live agent and could have been handled by Web or some other kind of self-service, or when the IVR scripts are not correct, and callers are being routed to the wrong skill group or department.

One example is trying to understand why customers are calling. Maybe they are saying "I tried to use the website, but it is down" or "The page isn't working," or "I've been transferred by another department," or "I couldn't find the right IVR option, so I've come through to you".

Another example is when the water company has a burst main. Rather than feed callers through to live agents, an IVR announcement and/or website notice can notify customers without the need to take up valuable live agent time.
